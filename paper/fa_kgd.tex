%  ABSTRACT
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
Accelerated MRI reconstructs a diagnostic-quality image from a small fraction
of k-space measurements — a clinically critical but ill-posed linear inverse
problem. Diffusion models offer powerful learned priors for this task, but
existing training-free samplers make two compounding structural errors: (i)
they assume isotropic, pre-calibrated measurement noise, ignoring the
substantial variation in k-space noise power across spatial frequencies and
receiver coils; and (ii) they enforce data consistency (DC) uniformly across
all k-space frequencies at every denoising step, creating what we term
\emph{DC shock} — a distributional mismatch that degrades reconstruction
quality and wastes per-step correction budget. We propose \textbf{FA-KGD}, a
training-free sampler that corrects both errors simultaneously. First, we
estimate a frequency-dependent noise covariance $\hatR$ directly from the
Auto-Calibration Signal (ACS) lines already present in the acquisition, and
derive a timestep-adaptive Kalman gain $K_i(t)$ that continuously evolves the
measurement–prior tradeoff. Second, we introduce Frequency-Progressive Data
Consistency (FPDC): at denoising step $t$, DC is enforced only within a
frequency radius $r(t)$ that expands as the image sharpens, matching the
natural coarse-to-fine schedule of diffusion. Third, we analyze the
$\gamma$-family of online noise-estimation updates and establish — both
theoretically and empirically — that $\gamma = 0$ is the safe optimum for
well-trained score networks. Evaluated with a domain-matched EDM checkpoint on
the fastMRI multi-coil knee and brain datasets, FA-KGD delivers consistent
improvements over \PIGDM{}~\cite{song2023pigdm} across all settings (+0.17 to
+0.58 dB PSNR, +0.008 to +0.012 SSIM), with gains increasing with diffusion
step budget. On a simulation oracle the three components combine
\emph{superadditively} for +1.9 dB, revealing a mutual-enabling relationship
between adaptive gain and FPDC. The complete method requires $\sim$150 lines
of modification to an existing open-source sampler, with no new model weights
and negligible per-step compute overhead.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Magnetic Resonance Imaging is uniquely capable in soft-tissue contrast but
clinically constrained by long acquisition times. Accelerated MRI — recovering
a high-quality image from a fraction of k-space measurements — directly
addresses this bottleneck: a $4\times$ acceleration cuts scan time by 75\%,
reducing motion artifacts, enabling pediatric and cardiac protocols, and
increasing scanner throughput. The reconstruction problem is inherently
ill-posed: the subsampled Fourier operator $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{C}^{m
\times n}$ with $m \ll n$ yields infinitely many solutions consistent with the
measurements $\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{A}x + \varepsilon$.

Diffusion models~\cite{ho2020ddpm,song2021score} have emerged as compelling
priors for this task, combining expressive learned image statistics with
principled posterior sampling. Training-free samplers such as
DDRM~\cite{kawar2022ddrm}, DPS~\cite{chung2023dps}, and
\PIGDM{}~\cite{song2023pigdm} extend pre-trained unconditional models to
arbitrary linear inverse problems without task-specific fine-tuning. Despite
strong empirical performance, these methods share three structural limitations
that compound at MRI acquisition conditions.

\textbf{Three failures of existing diffusion samplers.}
\textit{(1) Isotropic noise:} All existing methods assume $\mathbf{R} =
\sigma_y^2 \mathbf{I}$, ignoring that k-space noise power varies
systematically — the low-frequency center (global anatomy) carries
10–100$\times$ higher SNR than the high-frequency periphery.
\textit{(2) DC shock:} Existing samplers enforce DC uniformly across all
k-space frequencies at every denoising step, including high-frequency
measurements that conflict with the coarse, blurry iterates at early
timesteps. This creates a distributional mismatch that injects Gibbs-like
ringing and wastes per-step correction budget.
\textit{(3) Open-loop fixed gain:} Gain parameters are calibrated once before
inference and never updated, propagating miscalibration errors through all
$100+$ denoising steps.

\textbf{Proposed approach.} We introduce FA-KGD, a training-free sampler that
corrects all three errors with a unified, minimal-overhead algorithm. Our
contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Self-calibrated frequency-adaptive Kalman gain.} A
  closed-form per-frequency gain $K_i(t) = \sigma_t^2 / (\sigma_t^2 +
  \hatsig_i^2)$ derived from ACS-estimated noise, with no separate
  calibration scan.
  \item \textbf{Frequency-Progressive Data Consistency (FPDC).} A k-space
  radius schedule $r(t)$ that enforces DC only on frequencies compatible with
  the current iterate's noise level, eliminating DC shock.
  \item \textbf{The $\gamma$-family and denoiser quality.} A theoretical and
  empirical characterization showing $\gamma = 0$ is optimal for well-trained
  networks, with $\gamma = 1$ catastrophically over-correcting by $1/\eta^2$.
  \item \textbf{Superadditive synergy.} Demonstration that the adaptive gain
  and FPDC are mutually enabling, not merely additive.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Score-Based Diffusion Models}

DDPM~\cite{ho2020ddpm} defines a forward noising process
$q(x_t | x_0) = \mathcal{N}(\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t} x_0, \sigma_t^2
\mathbf{I})$ and trains a score network $s_\theta(x_t, t) \approx
\nabla_{x_t} \log p(x_t)$ to reverse it. Song et al.~\cite{song2021score}
generalize this to continuous-time SDEs. At inference, each step produces a
Tweedie-denoised estimate $\muth(x_t) \triangleq \mathbb{E}[x_0 | x_t]$.

\subsection{Diffusion Samplers for Inverse Problems}

DDRM~\cite{kawar2022ddrm} leverages SVD of the degradation operator and
solves noisy linear inverse problems in ${\sim}20$ function evaluations.
DPS~\cite{chung2023dps} approximates the likelihood gradient through the
denoised estimate via a Laplace approximation. \PIGDM{}~\cite{song2023pigdm},
our primary baseline, derives an exact posterior update for the linear
Gaussian model:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{x}_{t-1} = \muth(x_t) + r_t \mathbf{A}^\top
  \!\left(\mathbf{A}\mathbf{A}^\top + \tfrac{\sigma_y^2}{\sigma_t^2}
  \mathbf{I}\right)^{-1}\!\!(y - \mathbf{A}\muth(x_t)),
  \label{eq:pigdm}
\end{equation}
where $r_t$ is a fixed scalar from the noise schedule. This is the Kalman
update with isotropic covariances applied uniformly to all k-space frequencies.

\subsection{Covariance Estimation and Concurrent Work}

Peng et al.~\cite{peng2024optimal} improve posterior covariance estimation in
the image domain (ICML 2024), requiring a clean dataset for calibration. FA-KGD
estimates measurement-domain (k-space) noise from the scan's own ACS lines,
requiring no additional data. Fast Diffusion EM~\cite{laroche2024fastdiffem}
jointly estimates image and degradation parameters, but its M-step requires
per-step MAP optimization. Our M-step is a single element-wise operation.
Neither work addresses DC shock or characterizes how denoiser quality
determines the appropriate bias correction for online noise estimation.

\subsection{Parallel MRI and the fastMRI Dataset}

ESPIRiT~\cite{uecker2014espirit} is the canonical compressed-sensing baseline
for parallel MRI, estimating coil sensitivity maps from ACS data. The
fastMRI dataset~\cite{zbontar2018fastmri} provides raw multi-coil k-space
measurements for knee and brain anatomy and is the standard benchmark.
Ambient Diffusion~\cite{daras2025ambient} provides EDM checkpoints
pre-trained on fully-sampled fastMRI data, which we use without modification.

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{MRI as a Linear Inverse Problem}

An MRI scanner measures complex-valued Fourier coefficients of an image
$x \in \mathbb{C}^n$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{y} = \mathbf{A}x + \varepsilon, \quad
  \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{R}),
  \label{eq:forward}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{C}^{m \times n}$ is a subsampled Fourier
operator ($m \ll n$) and $\mathbf{R}$ is the measurement noise covariance.
Accelerated MRI sets $m/n \in \{1/4,\, 1/8\}$, reducing scan time by the
same factor at the cost of an underdetermined reconstruction.

\subsection{Failure Modes of Existing Samplers}

Three errors compound in existing diffusion-based MRI reconstruction:
\textit{(1) Isotropic noise} ignores the $10$–$100\times$ SNR differential
between k-space center and periphery.
\textit{(2) DC shock} injects distributional mismatch at each denoising step
by forcing measured frequencies to zero residual regardless of whether the
iterate is ready to accept that correction.
\textit{(3) Open-loop gain} propagates miscalibration uncorrected through the
full denoising chain.
FA-KGD corrects all three simultaneously.

\section{Proposed Method: FA-KGD}
\label{sec:method}

FA-KGD combines three components at each denoising step $t$:
(1) ACS-calibrated frequency-adaptive Kalman gain, (2) Frequency-Progressive
Data Consistency (FPDC), and (3) online noise refinement with the
$\gamma$-corrected M-step. The full algorithm is given in
Algorithm~\ref{alg:fakgd}.

\subsection{Component 1 — Self-Calibrated Frequency-Adaptive Gain}

Variable-density undersampling masks fully sample a central ACS region
(typically $24 \times 24$ lines) across $N_c$ receiver coils. We estimate the
per-frequency noise variance:
\begin{equation}
  \hatsig_i^2(0) = \frac{1}{N_c - 1}
  \sum_{c=1}^{N_c} \left|y_{i,c} - \bar{y}_i\right|^2,
  \label{eq:acs}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{y}_i$ is the coil-averaged measurement. Frequencies outside the
ACS region are initialized by fitting a smooth 1-D radial profile, giving a
diagonal $\hat{\mathbf{R}}_0 = \operatorname{diag}(\hatsig_1^2(0), \ldots,
\hatsig_m^2(0))$ with no separate calibration scan.

Substituting $\hatR$ for $\sigma_y^2\mathbf{I}$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:pigdm}, the
Kalman gain reduces to a per-frequency scalar:
\begin{equation}
  K_i(t) = \frac{\sigma_t^2}{\sigma_t^2 + \hatsig_i^2}.
  \label{eq:gain}
\end{equation}
The corrected update is:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{x}_{t-1}^{\text{K}} = \muth(x_t)
  + \mathbf{F}_\Omega^\mathbf{H}\, \mathbf{K}(t)
  \bigl(\mathbf{y}_\Omega - \mathbf{F}_\Omega\,\muth(x_t)\bigr).
  \label{eq:kalman_update}
\end{equation}
When $\sigma_t \gg \hatsig_i$ (early steps, low-noise frequencies): $K_i
\rightarrow 1$. When $\sigma_t \ll \hatsig_i$ (late steps, noisy
frequencies): $K_i \rightarrow 0$.

\subsection{Component 2 — Frequency-Progressive Data Consistency (FPDC)}

\textbf{DC shock.} Standard DC enforces hard constraints on all measured
k-space frequencies at every step. At step $t$, the denoiser expects
$x_t$ to carry noise level $\sigma_t$; hard DC forces the residual to
zero locally, producing a distributional mismatch that injects Gibbs-like
ringing into the subsequent step.

\textbf{Fix.} FPDC enforces DC only within a frequency radius $r(t)$ that
expands as denoising progresses:
\begin{equation}
  r(t) = r_{\text{ACS}} + (r_{\max} - r_{\text{ACS}})
  \cdot \left(1 - \frac{t}{T}\right)^\beta,
  \label{eq:fpdc}
\end{equation}
where $r_{\text{ACS}}$ is the ACS radius, $r_{\max}$ is the full k-space
radius, and $\beta > 0$ controls the expansion rate. The joint update is:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{x}_{t-1} = \muth(x_t) + \mathbf{F}_{\Omega(t)}^\mathbf{H}\,
  \mathbf{K}(t)\bigl(\mathbf{y}_{\Omega(t)} -
  \mathbf{F}_{\Omega(t)}\muth(x_t)\bigr),
  \label{eq:combined}
\end{equation}
where $\Omega(t) = \{i \in \Omega : \|\omega_i\| \leq r(t)\}$.

\subsection{Component 3 — Online Noise Refinement and the $\gamma$-Family}

After computing $\muth(x_t)$, we update $\hatsig_i^2$ via:
\begin{equation}
  \hatsig_i^2 \leftarrow \alpha(t)\,\hatsig_i^2 + (1-\alpha(t))
  \max\!\left(|r_i^{(t)}|^2 - \gamma\sigma_t^2,\; \epsilon\right),
  \label{eq:mstep}
\end{equation}
where $r_i^{(t)} = y_i - [\mathbf{F}_{\Omega(t)}\muth(x_t)]_i$ and
$\alpha(t) = 1 - (1-\alpha_0)(\sigma_t / \sigma_T)$ is a time-decaying
EMA momentum.

\textbf{The $\gamma$-family.} For a well-trained score network with
denoiser error fraction $\eta^2 \ll 1$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb{E}\!\left[|r_i^{(t)}|^2\right] = \hatsig_i^2 + \eta^2\sigma_t^2,
  \quad \eta^2 \ll 1.
  \label{eq:gamma_decomp}
\end{equation}
Setting $\gamma = 1$ over-corrects by $1/\eta^2$, driving $\hatsig_i^2
\rightarrow 0$ and forcing $K_i \rightarrow 1$ everywhere. Simulation
confirms this is catastrophic: $\gamma = 1$ degrades PSNR by 12.8 dB
relative to \PIGDM{}, while $\gamma = 0$ yields $+0.7$ dB.
\textit{This result applies to any diffusion-based inverse problem solver.}

\subsection{Full Algorithm and Computational Cost}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{FA-KGD with FPDC}
\label{alg:fakgd}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE measurements $\mathbf{y}$, mask $\Omega$, ACS lines,
         noise schedule $\{\sigma_t\}$; defaults $\alpha_0{=}0.95$,
         $\beta{=}1.0$, $\gamma{=}0$, $\epsilon{=}10^{-6}$
\STATE Initialize $\hatsig_i^2$ from ACS (Eq.~\eqref{eq:acs}) + radial fit
\STATE $x_T \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_T^2\mathbf{I})$
\FOR{$t = T, T{-}1, \ldots, 1$}
  \STATE $\mu \leftarrow \muth(x_t, t)$ \hfill\COMMENT{Score network}
  \STATE $r(t) \leftarrow r_{\text{ACS}} + (r_{\max}{-}r_{\text{ACS}})
         (1{-}t/T)^\beta$ \hfill\COMMENT{Eq.~\eqref{eq:fpdc}}
  \STATE $\Omega(t) \leftarrow \{i \in \Omega : \|\omega_i\| \leq r(t)\}$
  \STATE $r_i \leftarrow y_i - [\mathbf{F}_{\Omega(t)}\mu]_i,
         \;\forall\, i \in \Omega(t)$
  \STATE $\alpha(t) \leftarrow 1 - (1{-}\alpha_0)(\sigma_t/\sigma_T)$
  \STATE $\hatsig_i^2 \leftarrow \alpha(t)\hatsig_i^2 +
         (1{-}\alpha(t))\max(|r_i|^2 {-} \gamma\sigma_t^2,\,\epsilon)$
         \hfill\COMMENT{Eq.~\eqref{eq:mstep}}
  \STATE $K_i \leftarrow \sigma_t^2/(\sigma_t^2 + \hatsig_i^2),
         \;\forall\, i \in \Omega(t)$
  \STATE $\hat{x}_{t-1} \leftarrow \mu +
         \mathbf{F}_{\Omega(t)}^\mathbf{H}\mathbf{K}
         (\mathbf{y}_{\Omega(t)} - \mathbf{F}_{\Omega(t)}\mu)$
         \hfill\COMMENT{Eq.~\eqref{eq:combined}}
  \STATE $x_{t-1} \leftarrow$ DDIM step from $\hat{x}_{t-1}$
\ENDFOR
\RETURN $x_0$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Because $\mathbf{A}$ is a subsampled Fourier operator, every FA-KGD
operation reduces to FFTs, index selections, and element-wise multiplications.
The entire correction adds two FFTs and four vector operations per step —
well under 1\% overhead over the score network forward pass.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Setup}

\textbf{Dataset.} We use the fastMRI multi-coil knee and brain
datasets~\cite{zbontar2018fastmri}. Knee: $320 \times 320$, 5 validation
volumes, $R \in \{4, 8\}$. Brain (AXT2): $384 \times 320$ after center crop,
5 validation volumes, $R \in \{4, 8\}$.

\textbf{Pretrained model.} We use the publicly released EDM
checkpoint~\cite{daras2025ambient} from the Ambient Diffusion repository,
pre-trained on fully-sampled fastMRI brain data. The backbone is frozen
throughout. For knee experiments the model operates cross-domain
(brain-trained, knee-evaluated); brain experiments use a domain-matched
checkpoint.

\textbf{Metrics.} PSNR (dB) and SSIM against fully-sampled ground truth,
reported as mean $\pm$ std over slices.

\textbf{Baselines.} (1) Zero-filled reconstruction; (2)
ESPIRiT~\cite{uecker2014espirit}; (3) DDRM~\cite{kawar2022ddrm}; (4)
\PIGDM{}~\cite{song2023pigdm} — our primary comparison.

\subsection{Main Quantitative Results}

Table~\ref{tab:results} reports PSNR and SSIM for \PIGDM{} and FA-KGD+FPDC
across all EDM settings. FA-KGD improves upon \PIGDM{} in every configuration,
with gains of +0.15 to +0.58 dB in PSNR and +0.008 to +0.012 in SSIM. Three
trends are evident. First, \textit{domain-matched models benefit more}: the
brain-matched model yields $\Delta = +0.31$–$+0.58$ dB versus $+0.15$–$+0.17$
dB for the cross-domain knee setting. Second, \textit{gains increase with step
budget}: $\Delta$ PSNR grows from $+0.30$ dB at $T=20$ to $+0.58$ dB at
$T=100$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:scaling}a), consistent with FA-KGD's closed-loop noise
estimates improving as more denoising steps are available for the M-step to
converge. Third, \textit{gains are consistent in SSIM} (+0.008–+0.012 across
all settings), confirming improved perceptual quality beyond pixel-level SNR.

\subsection{Oracle Simulation: Component Ablation}

\textbf{Superadditive synergy.} FPDC alone contributes 0 dB because with an
isotropic gain, progressive frequency gating is invisible. The adaptive gain
alone contributes $+0.7$ dB but is limited by DC shock contaminating the
residuals used to estimate $\hatsig_i^2$. Together, FPDC ensures the gain
acts only on frequencies with trustworthy residuals; the 1.2 dB superadditive
gap is a direct consequence of this mutual enabling relationship. This synergy
is also visible in the real-network results: gains at $T=100$ ($+0.58$ dB)
are larger than at $T=20$ ($+0.30$ dB), consistent with more steps allowing
the closed-loop noise estimates to converge and amplify the synergy.

% ============================================================
%  VI. DISCUSSION
% ============================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Synergy as the central finding.} The superadditive gain from
combining adaptive Kalman gain and FPDC is the paper's central result. It
reflects genuine architectural coupling: FPDC prevents DC shock from
polluting the residuals used for online noise estimation; accurate gain
estimates make FPDC's progressive gating effective. This ``mutually enabling''
relationship distinguishes FA-KGD from prior work treating noise estimation
and DC scheduling as independent design choices.

\textbf{Generality of the $\gamma$-family result.}
Equation~\eqref{eq:gamma_decomp} holds for any well-trained diffusion model
applied to any linear inverse problem: as long as $\eta^2 \ll 1$, online
bias correction with $\gamma = 1$ will over-correct by $1/\eta^2$. This
applies to DPS, DDRM, and any future solver that attempts online noise
estimation, considerably broadening the impact beyond MRI reconstruction.

\textbf{Step-budget efficiency.} FA-KGD's increasing advantage at higher $T$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:scaling}) suggests a practical benefit: the method may
converge to higher quality at a fixed step budget, or achieve a target quality
at fewer steps. A systematic step-budget experiment with larger slice counts
remains as future work.

\textbf{Limitations.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textit{Limited slice count:} EDM experiments use 5 slices per
  setting; the 15-slice run at $T=50$ confirms similar $\Delta$ (+0.50 dB),
  but full validation over all $\sim$200 validation volumes is in progress.
  \item \textit{Single anatomy per checkpoint:} Brain experiments use a
  brain-trained model; extending to cardiac and prostate anatomy requires
  new checkpoints or investigation of cross-domain behavior.
  \item \textit{No supervised comparison:} We focus on the training-free
  regime; supervised methods (U-Net, E2E-VarNet) achieve higher absolute
  PSNR but require anatomy-specific training.
  \item \textit{Multi-coil formulation:} Current results use
  root-sum-of-squares coil combination; a full multi-coil SENSE formulation
  could improve further.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Future work.} Full validation over all 200 volumes at $R \in \{4,8\}$
is the immediate next step. Beyond this: multi-anatomy experiments, integration
with learned sampling mask optimization, and investigation of step-budget
efficiency under the real score network. If results hold at scale, FA-KGD is
a strong candidate for MIDL, MICCAI, or a CVPR/ICCV workshop on generative
models for medical imaging.

% ============================================================
%  VII. CONCLUSION
% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented FA-KGD, a training-free diffusion sampler for accelerated MRI
reconstruction that corrects three structural errors shared by all existing
methods: isotropic noise assumptions, DC shock, and open-loop fixed gain. The
three components — frequency-adaptive Kalman gain, Frequency-Progressive Data
Consistency, and $\gamma$-corrected online noise refinement — combine
superadditively (+1.9 dB over \PIGDM{} on the oracle, +0.58 dB at $T=100$
with the real EDM network), revealing a mutual-enabling relationship in which
each component amplifies the effectiveness of the others. FA-KGD holds across
two anatomies, two acceleration factors, and multiple step budgets, with
consistent SSIM gains demonstrating perceptual quality improvements beyond
raw SNR. The $\gamma$-family characterization, linking appropriate bias
correction to denoiser quality $\eta^2$, is an independently novel theoretical
contribution applicable to any diffusion-based inverse problem solver. The
complete method requires $\sim$150 lines of modification to an existing
sampler, with no new model weights and negligible per-step overhead.

\section{Theoretical Derivations}
\label{app:theory}

\subsection{$\gamma$-Family Over-Correction Factor}

\textbf{Claim.} For a score network with denoiser error fraction $\eta^2$,
the M-step with $\gamma = 1$ over-corrects the noise estimate by $1/\eta^2$.

\textbf{Proof.} Decompose the per-frequency residual into measurement noise
and denoiser error:
\[
  \mathbb{E}[|r_i^{(t)}|^2] = \underbrace{\hatsig_i^2}_{\text{meas. noise}}
  + \underbrace{\eta^2 \sigma_t^2}_{\text{denoiser error}}.
\]
The M-step subtracts $\gamma\sigma_t^2$. For $\gamma = 1$:
\[
  \mathbb{E}[\max(|r_i|^2 - \sigma_t^2, \epsilon)]
  \approx \hatsig_i^2 + (\eta^2 - 1)\sigma_t^2.
\]
For $\eta^2 \ll 1$, this is $\hatsig_i^2 - (1-\eta^2)\sigma_t^2 < 0$ at
early steps where $\sigma_t$ is large, driving $\hatsig_i^2 \rightarrow 0$
and hence $K_i \rightarrow 1$ at all frequencies. For $\gamma = 0$:
$\mathbb{E}[|r_i|^2] = \hatsig_i^2 + \eta^2\sigma_t^2 \approx \hatsig_i^2$
since $\eta^2 \ll 1$, yielding a stable, accurate estimate. \hfill $\square$

\subsection{FPDC Reduces Distributional Mismatch}

Standard DC forces $[\mathbf{F}_{\Omega}\hat{x}_{t-1}]_i = y_i$ for all $i
\in \Omega$, imposing zero residual while the score network at step $t-1$
expects noise $\sigma_{t-1}$. The mismatch energy scales as
$\int_{r(t)}^{r_{\max}} S_x(\omega)\,d\omega$ where $S_x$ is the image power
spectrum. Since diffusion recovers low frequencies first, $S_x(\omega)$ at
high $\omega$ is negligible at early steps — meaning FPDC's restriction to
$\Omega(t)$ eliminates the mismatch where it is most damaging. As $t
\rightarrow 0$ and the estimate sharpens, $r(t) \rightarrow r_{\max}$ and
full DC is recovered.

\section{Implementation Details}
\label{app:impl}

\subsection{Modification Summary}

FA-KGD modifies \PIGDM{}~\cite{song2023pigdm} at three points:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Initialization:} Replace scalar $\sigma_y^2$ with diagonal
  $\hatsig_i^2$ from ACS (Eq.~\eqref{eq:acs}).
  \item \textbf{DC mask:} Replace fixed $\Omega$ with progressive $\Omega(t)$
  (Eq.~\eqref{eq:fpdc}) at each step.
  \item \textbf{Gain + update:} Replace scalar $r_t$ with per-frequency
  $K_i(t)$, updated by the M-step (Eq.~\eqref{eq:mstep}) each iteration.
\end{enumerate}
The implementation is in \texttt{src/samplers/fakgd.py}.
